#Defining import function

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns

def import_stats(batting_csv,pitching_csv,players_csv):                             #Enter Files and adjusts stats
    players= pd.read_csv(players_csv,\
                    usecols=['playerID','nameLast','nameGiven','debut',"finalGame"]).copy()     # .copy to make sure we do not change original.
    batting= pd.read_csv(batting_csv).copy()
    batting.drop(['yearID','stint','teamID','lgID'],1,inplace=True)                                # Drop some less important stats.  
    pitching=pd.read_csv(pitching_csv).copy()
    pitching.drop(['yearID','IBB','GF','stint','teamID','lgID','SH','SF','GIDP'],1,inplace=True)   # Drop some less important stats. 
    pitching_avg=pitching[['ERA','BAOpp','playerID']].groupby('playerID',as_index=False).mean()    # average stats needed
    pitching.drop(['ERA','BAOpp'],1,inplace=True)                                                  #Get rid of these from DF
    pitching_sums=pitching.groupby('playerID',as_index=False).sum()
    pitching_totals=pitching_sums.merge(pitching_avg,on='playerID',how='inner')                    # Pitching stats summed or averaged
    batting_sums=batting.groupby('playerID',as_index=False).sum()                                  #All batting stats summed
    pitching_stats = players.merge(pitching_totals, on='playerID',how='inner')                     #Merge players with stats
    batting_stats =players.merge(batting_sums, on='playerID',how='inner')
    return (batting_stats, pitching_stats)

The function above was defined to give us a worakable Pandas Dataframe for our batting and pitching statistics. Since we are investigating the player's career stats with their career salary earnings, we will use the above function to import the statistics and create two seaperate Dataframes, one for pitchers and the other for batters. These players were seaperated because of the nature of the game of baseball, and the complete lack of overlap between bating and pitching statistics. What the function will do, is import the CSV files found from our source, and create two dataframes, but the dataframes will have all of the players personal info as well as the batting or pitching information. def find_corr(df,stat):
    corr= df.corr()[stat].sort_values(ascending=False)
    corr=corr.iloc[1:5],corr.tail(5)
    return corr

After running this for the first time we can see a couple of things.

For pitchers, it seems that the cumliative stats, like "Strikeouts", and "Wins" have the most correlation with players overall career earnings. The more strikeouts and wins a player accumilates over a career, the higher their career earnings will most likely be. The correlation coefficient for strikeouts was the highest correlation calculated to career salary, and it had a value of r=.680.

The negative correlation with "Earned Runed Average", and "Opponents Batting Average" makes snese, because these two stats are averages about how many runs and hits opponents have against them, which means that the lower these are, the better the pitcher (in theory). It is shocking however, that these two stats do not have a greater absolute value, because these stats are often considered very important in determining the ability of a pitcher. I would have expected these to be larger negative numbers, instead of just r=-.078 and r=-.114, respectively. Thus we will assume that because the absolute values of these correlations is smaller than the absolute value of the top stats' correlation coefficients, that ERA and Opponents Batting Average are less correlated to a player's career earnings then the top stats.

For batters, "Home Runs" are the most telling stat of correlation to a players' career earnings, with "Runs Batted In" a close second. Also, "Sacrafice Hits" and "Caught Steeling" stats are the least predictive of career earnings, which I believe makes sense, because these stats are often not seen as crucial stats for judging a player's skills. This correlation coefficent calculated for the number of Home Runs a player hits over his career and his salary earnings was calculated to be r=.566.

Results and Further Questions¶

One thing that stuck out in this first analysis is that cummlitative stats have a higher relationfhip to player's career earnings than other factors. This really stood out when I saw that for pitchers, the more home runs the pitcher had given up, the higher their career earnings. Giving up home runs is a negative attribute for pitchers, but the fact that this stat was the fourth highest stat correlated to pitchers' salaries made me realize this was worth investigating.

One though that became clear, is that this may be due to the fact that we were comparing TOTAL career earnings to other player stats. It is clear that players who play longer, are more likely to gain all additive stats (wins, loses, home runs against, ect.) as well as earn more money, because they will simply have more statistics ovarall and will have more years of getting paid. Thus this may be a slightly biased analysis, and we may need to revise these calculation. Thus it is worth investingating to see if switching to average salaries and not just total salaries will make a difference in the correlation between stats.

Also one missing stat that I was surprised not to see is in the batting statistics CSV file in general was Batting Average; argueably the most refered to stat in baseball for hitters. Thus I also want to add this stat, to see if there is a clear comparision between Batting Averages and earnings, and if this was more significant than Home Runs hit. Batting average is a simple calculation of taking the total number of hits a player has recorded and dividng that number by the total number of at bats.

Conclusions and Suggestions¶

If you are a baseball player, and you want to earn more in your career, it clearly seems that it is more likely to happen if you hit home runs as a batter, or throw strike outs as a pitcher. These stats seem to be the most correlated to higher career earnings than stats like games played or even games won. This analysis shows that these stats are the most related to high career salary earnings, and that other stats, even ones assumed to be important like Batting Average, may not be as importnat to a higher salary. While these stats are the most correlated to palyers career salaries, there is still much room for further investigation.

While this paper did not look at this, it may be worth examining players's salaries on a normalized scale. Inflation, and the fact that these statistics come from players all the way from 1871, there may be different results if the salary scales were normalized, or factored with the value of a salary for the time. Also this analysis did not take any look into the social changes in players' contracts over the long history of baseball. So while there was an investigation of how player stats are correlated to career earnings, futuer analysis may look into researching how contracts, salaries, and inflation as affected baseball players earnings.
