import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

def import_stats(batting_csv,pitching_csv,players_csv):                             
    players= pd.read_csv(players_csv,\
                    usecols=['playerID','nameLast','nameGiven',\
                             'debut',"finalGame"]).copy()     # .copy to make sure we do not change original.
    batting= pd.read_csv(batting_csv).copy()
    batting.drop(['yearID','stint','teamID','lgID'],1,inplace=True)                                # Drop some less important stats.  
    pitching=pd.read_csv(pitching_csv).copy()
    pitching.drop(['yearID','IBB','GF','stint','teamID','lgID','SH','SF','GIDP'],1,inplace=True)   # Drop some less important stats. 
    pitching_avg=pitching[['ERA','BAOpp','playerID']].groupby('playerID',as_index=False).mean()    # average stats needed
    pitching.drop(['ERA','BAOpp'],1,inplace=True)                                                  #Get rid of these from DF
    pitching_sums=pitching.groupby('playerID',as_index=False).sum()
    pitching_totals=pitching_sums.merge(pitching_avg,on='playerID',how='inner')                    # Pitching stats summed or averaged
    batting_sums=batting.groupby('playerID',as_index=False).sum()                                  #All batting stats summed
    pitching_stats = players.merge(pitching_totals, on='playerID',how='inner')                     #Merge players with stats
    batting_stats =players.merge(batting_sums, on='playerID',how='inner')
    return (batting_stats, pitching_stats)

The function above was defined to give us a worakable Pandas Dataframe for our batting and pitching statistics. Since we are investigating the player's career stats with their career salary earnings, we will use the above function to import the statistics and create two seaperate Dataframes, one for pitchers and the other for batters. These players were seaperated because of the nature of the game of baseball, and the complete lack of overlap between bating and pitching statistics. What the function will do, is import the CSV files found from our source, and create dataframes of the players' personal info as well as the batting or pitching performance statistics. def find_corr(df,stat):
    corr= df.corr()[stat].sort_values(ascending=False)
    corr=corr.iloc[1:5],corr.tail(5)
    return corr

After running this for the first time we can see a couple of things.

For pitchers, it seems that the cumliative stats, like "Strikeouts", and "Wins" have the highest correlation to career earnings. The more strikeouts and wins a player accumilates over a career, the higher their career earnings will most likely be. The correlation coefficient for strikeouts has a value of r=.680, which is a fairly strong correlation.

The negative correlation with "Earned Runed Average", and "Opponents Batting Average" makes snese, because these two stats are averages about how many runs and hits opponents have against them, which means that the lower these are, the better the pitcher (in theory). It is shocking however, that these two stats do not have a greater absolute value, because these stats are often considered very important in determining the ability of a pitcher. I would have expected these to be larger negative numbers, instead of the weak correlations of just r=-.078 and r=-.114, respectively.

For batters, "Home Runs" are the most telling stat of correlation to a players' career earnings, with "Runs Batted In" a close second. Also, "Sacrafice Hits" and "Caught Steeling" stats are the least predictive of career earnings, which I believe makes sense, because these stats are often not seen as crucial stats for judging a player's skills. This correlation coefficent calculated for the number of Home Runs a player hits over his career and his salary earnings was calculated to be r=.566.

Further Questions¶

So Home Runs and Strikeouts are the most crucial statstics for baseball players who want to earn more money! Obviously with fans so excited to see pitchers who strike opponents out and batters who can hit the long ball, it seems to make sense that players who earn more have more strikeouts or home runs! There are some more questions worth addressing though before we can be convinced of the importance of these two stats.

One thing that stuck out in this first analysis is that cummlitative stats have a higher relationfhip to player's career earnings than other factors. Even when looking at negative stats like the "home runs against" a pitcher, the more home runs a pitcher had given up, the higher that player's career earnings. The fact that this stat has the fourth highest correlation to pitchers' salaries made me realize this was worth investigating.

Because we are comparing TOTAL career earnings to other stats, players who play longer are more likely to gain all additive stats (wins, loses, home runs against, ect.). Since this could possibly be a baised stat, let's investigate the relationship between Annual Average Salary and the rest of a player's statistics. This could possibly give us different correlation calculations. Thus we will adjust this and recheck the analysis.

Thus I also want to calculate this statistic by hand, to see if there is a high correlation between Batting Averages and career earnings. I believe batting averages may be more correlated to palyers earning money than Home Runs. Conclusions and Suggestions¶

If you are a baseball player, and you want to earn more in your career, it clearly seems that it is more likely to happen if you hit home runs as a batter, or throw strike outs as a pitcher. These stats seem to be the most correlated to higher career earnings than others like games played or even games won. While these stats are the most correlated to palyers' career salaries, there is still much room for further investigation.

This simply summed career salaries, so it may be worth examining players's salaries on a normalized scale, or with some other factors into player salary. The statistics in this paper come from players all the way from 1871, but this paper did not account for ecconomic factors like inflation. There may be different results if the salary scales were normalized, or factored with the value of a salary for the time. Also this analysis did not take a look into the social changes in players' contracts over the long history of baseball. So while there was an investigation of how player stats are correlated to career earnings, futuer analysis may look into researching how contracts, salaries, and inflation have affected baseball players earnings.
